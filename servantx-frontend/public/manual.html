Platform Overview

ServantX is an AI-powered medical billing audit platform that automatically detects insurance underpayments and generates ready-to-file appeal letters. By combining CMS 2026 Medicare fee schedules with your payer contracts, ServantX reprices every remittance line item and flags discrepancies with plain-English explanations and confidence scores.

Who this manual is for: Hospital billing departments, revenue cycle managers, practice managers, and coding specialists who upload remittance files, review findings, and manage payer appeals.

Section 2.0

Hospital Settings & Pricing Configuration

Pricing configuration is the most critical setup step. It tells ServantX which fee schedule engines to use when repricing claims from your 835 files. Choosing the wrong mode will produce misleading variance results. Read this section carefully before uploading remittance files.

Navigate to: /dashboard/settings → Pricing Config tab

2.1 Hospital Profile Settings

  1. Click your username or the settings gear icon in the top navigation bar.
  2. Select Settings from the dropdown menu.
  3. On the Settings page, confirm or update your Hospital Name, NPI (if applicable), and Contact Information.
  4. Click Save Profile.

2.2 Pricing Mode — Overview

ServantX uses multiple fee schedule engines to determine the "correct" reimbursement for each claim. The Pricing Mode controls which engines are activated and in what order. The platform reads claim data from each 835 file — including payer type, procedure codes (CPT/HCPCS), and place of service — and selects the appropriate repricing logic.

The Three Medicare Engines Explained

MPFS — Medicare Physician Fee Schedule
Applies to professional/physician claims billed on a CMS-1500 form. Covers office visits, procedures, specialist consultations, lab interpretations, and other outpatient physician services. Uses RVU-based pricing with GPCI geographic adjustments.
IPPS — Inpatient Prospective Payment System
Applies to hospital inpatient admissions billed on a UB-04 form with a Diagnosis Related Group (DRG) code. Payment is based on the DRG weight × base rate. Used for acute care hospital stays.
OPPS/APC — Outpatient Prospective Payment System
Applies to hospital outpatient services: emergency department visits, same-day surgery, infusion therapy, imaging, observation stays, and clinic visits billed by a facility. Claims are classified into Ambulatory Payment Classification (APC) groups for payment.

Selecting Your Pricing Mode

Mode Engine Behavior Best Use Case
AUTO Recommended Reads payer type from the 835. Medicare claims → MPFS/IPPS/OPPS (by claim type). Medicaid → state fee schedule. Commercial → contract + AI fallback. Default setting for most hospitals with a mixed payer mix. Best starting point.
MEDICARE Forces all claims through CMS 2026 Medicare fee schedules regardless of actual payer listed in the 835. Medicare-focused audits; benchmarking commercial payer rates against Medicare as a baseline.
MEDICAID Forces claims through Texas Medicaid Fee-For-Service rates. Claims for other states fall through to CONTRACT + AI. Texas Medicaid audits. Note: non-Texas Medicaid states are not yet natively supported.
CONTRACT Uses only the rules extracted from your uploaded payer contract, with AI gap-filling for missing codes. When you have a comprehensive, recently uploaded contract with full CPT-level fee schedules.
ALL Runs all available engines simultaneously. The engine that produces the highest-variance result at ≥30% confidence is selected for the finding. Maximum recovery potential; QA validation to compare engines; compliance audits where you need full documentation.

2.3 Configuring Pricing Mode (Step-by-Step)

  1. Navigate to /dashboard/settings and click the Pricing Config tab.
  2. In the Pricing Mode dropdown, select the appropriate mode for your audit objective. If unsure, select AUTO.
  3. In the State field, enter your two-letter state code (e.g., TX for Texas). This is required for Medicaid mode and improves AUTO mode accuracy.
  4. Review the engine summary that appears below the dropdown — it describes what will happen when you upload 835 files.
  5. Click Save Pricing Configuration.

Section 3.0

Payer Contracts

Payer contracts define your negotiated reimbursement rates with commercial insurers. Uploading your contracts allows ServantX to audit commercial claims against your actual agreed-upon rates — not just a generic benchmark. The better your contract coverage, the more precise your audit findings.

Navigate to: /dashboard/contracts

3.1 What ServantX Extracts from Contracts

The AI contract parser reads your PDF or DOCX file and automatically extracts:

  • Fee schedule entries — CPT/HCPCS code-level rates or percentage-of-billed-charge rules
  • Percentage-of-billed rules — e.g., "85% of billed charges for unlisted procedures"
  • Carve-outs and special provisions — services with non-standard payment rules
  • IPPS/OPPS rates — facility-specific inpatient/outpatient addenda
  • Payment timelines — timely payment provisions and interest clauses
  • Timely filing deadlines — the window within which claims and appeals must be submitted
About the Demo Contract
New accounts include a pre-loaded synthetic demonstration contract. This contract does not contain a CPT-level fee schedule — it is for familiarization only. For accurate audit results, you must upload your actual payer contracts before processing production 835 files.

3.2 Uploading a Payer Contract

  1. Navigate to /dashboard/contracts.
  2. Click Upload Contract (top-right button).
  3. Select your contract file. Supported formats: PDF or DOCX. Maximum file size: 50 MB.
  4. In the Payer Name field, enter the exact payer name (e.g., "Aetna," "BlueCross BlueShield of Texas," "UnitedHealthcare"). This must match the payer identifier in your 835 files for automatic rule matching.
  5. Click Upload & Process. The AI extraction process begins immediately.
  6. Wait for the status to change from Processing to Extracted. Large contracts may take 2–5 minutes.
  7. Once complete, click on the contract to review the extracted Rule Library.

3.3 Reviewing Extracted Rules

  1. From /dashboard/contracts, click on a contract to open it.
  2. Review the Rule Library — a structured table of every extracted rate, rule, and provision.
  3. Spot-check several CPT codes you know well against your paper contract. Verify the rates match.

Tip — Improve Coverage
If coverage is lower than expected, check whether your contract has attached fee schedule addenda (separate PDF pages or Excel tables). Re-upload with the addenda included as pages within the same PDF. Alternatively, use the Reprocess function after combining documents.

    • "Does this contract have any mental health carve-outs?"

  4. The AI responds with a direct answer and cites the relevant contract section or page number.

3.5 Reprocessing a Contract

If you believe the initial extraction missed rules (e.g., the contract was scanned and the OCR was imperfect, or you have since combined addenda), you can trigger a fresh extraction.

  1. Open the contract from /dashboard/contracts.
  2. Click the Reprocess button (or the three-dot menu → Reprocess).

Section 4.0

Uploading 835 Remittance Files

The 835 Electronic Remittance Advice (ERA) is the standard file payers use to explain every payment and adjustment they make to your claims. This is the primary data source for ServantX auditing. Most clearinghouses and practice management systems can export these files on demand.

Navigate to: /dashboard/receipts

4.1 What Is an 835 File?

The 835 is an EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) X12 transaction set defined by the HIPAA standard. Each 835 file contains one or more claim payment records, with detailed breakdowns of:

  • The original billed amount for each service line
  • The amount the payer actually paid
  • Adjustment reason codes (why payment was reduced)
  • Contractual adjustments, patient responsibility, and denials
  • Payer identification and claim identifiers

Where to Get Your 835 Files

Source How to Export
Availity Remittance Viewer → Remittance Files → Download ERA (select date range and payer)
Change Healthcare Enrollment & Connectivity → ERA Downloads → Export .835
Waystar Remittance → ERA Management → Download as 835 File
Practice Management System Varies by system — look for "ERA Export," "835 Export," or "Remittance Download" in your billing module
Direct from Payer Some payers (e.g., Noridian for Medicare) offer portal downloads; file type will be .835 or .txt
File Extensions
835 files may have extensions: .835, .txt, .edi, or .x12. ServantX accepts all of these. The file content must be a valid X12 835 transaction set.

4.2 Uploading 835 Files

  1. Navigate to /dashboard/receipts.
  2. Click Upload Files.
  3. Choose your upload method:
    • Single file — select one .835 file from your computer
    • Bulk upload — hold Ctrl (Windows) or Command (Mac) and select multiple .835 files
    • ZIP archive — compress multiple 835 files into a .zip and upload the archive; ServantX automatically unpacks and processes each file
  4. Click Upload. Files are validated and queued immediately.
  5. Monitor the status column on the Receipts page. Processing typically completes within 1–3 minutes per file depending on size.

4.3 What Happens After Upload

ServantX processes each uploaded file through a multi-stage pipeline:

  1. File validation — the 835 structure is verified against the X12 standard. Invalid files are flagged with an error message.
  2. Deduplication check — a SHA-256 cryptographic hash of the file content is compared against previously uploaded files. Exact duplicates are rejected to prevent double-counting.
  3. Claim extraction — each service line is parsed out: CPT/HCPCS codes, payer ID, billed amount, paid amount, adjustment codes, date of service, rendering provider, and DRG (if inpatient).
  4. Repricing engine selection — based on your Pricing Mode setting and the payer/claim type, the appropriate fee schedule engine (MPFS, IPPS, OPPS, CONTRACT, or AI) is selected.
  5. Repricing & variance calculation — every claim is repriced. Variance = Expected Amount − Paid Amount. Positive variance = potential underpayment.
  6. Confidence scoring — the AI assigns a 0–100 confidence score based on data completeness, fee schedule match quality, and rule applicability.
  7. Document creation — an Audit Document is created for each claim and made available in /dashboard/documents.
Batch Size Limit
Each upload batch processes a maximum of 30 claims in a single run. Larger 835 files are automatically chunked. If you upload a file with 200 claims, ServantX will process it in multiple batches automatically — all resulting documents will appear in the Documents view.

4.4 Managing Uploaded Receipts

  • View receipts list — the Receipts page shows all uploaded files with upload date, status, and claim count.
  • Open a receipt — click any row to see the raw claim list extracted from that file.
  • File status indicators:
    • Queued — waiting to be processed
    • Processing — currently being analyzed
    • Complete — all claims processed; documents available
    • Error — validation failed; check the error message and re-upload a corrected file
Section 5.0

Audit Documents & Findings

Every claim extracted from your 835 files becomes an Audit Document. This is your primary workspace for reviewing findings, evaluating underpayments, and deciding which claims are worth appealing.

Navigate to: /dashboard/documents

5.1 Anatomy of an Audit Document

Click any document row to open the full detail view. Each document contains:

Field Description
Claim Summary Payer name, date of service, rendering provider, place of service
CPT/HCPCS Codes All procedure codes billed on this claim, with descriptions
Billed Amount The amount your organization submitted to the payer
Paid Amount The amount the payer actually remitted per the 835
Expected Amount What ServantX calculated the payer should have paid per the applicable fee schedule or contract
Variance Expected − Paid. A positive number indicates a potential underpayment.
Pricing Engine Used Which engine produced the finding: MPFS, IPPS, OPPS, CONTRACT_AUDIT, or AI
Confidence Score 0–100 scale. Higher = stronger basis for the finding.
AI Reasoning Plain-English explanation of why this was flagged and how the expected amount was calculated
Multi-Engine Table Side-by-side comparison of all engines (visible when ALL mode was used)

5.2 Understanding Confidence Scores

The confidence score reflects how well the pricing engine was able to match your claim to authoritative rate data. It is not a judgment of whether the payer made an error — it is a measure of data quality.

80–100
Strong case — confident match to fee schedule. File appeal with high priority.
50–79
Moderate confidence — review AI reasoning before deciding to file.
30–49
Lower confidence — may reflect a gap in contract coverage. Manual review strongly recommended.
0–29
Insufficient data — do not file an appeal based on this finding alone.
Working Efficiently
Start your appeal queue with documents at 80+ confidence and a variance above your minimum threshold (e.g., $50). Small-dollar, low-confidence findings cost more to appeal than you recover.

5.3 Document Status Flow

Every document has a processing status (distinct from appeal status) that shows whether the AI analysis is complete:

not_submitted → in_progress → succeeded
failed — AI processing error; check the error details and contact support if it persists.

5.4 Filtering and Searching Documents

  1. Navigate to /dashboard/documents.
  2. Use the Filter bar to narrow results by:
    • Appeal status (none, identified, drafted, filed, approved, etc.)
    • Payer name
    • Date range
    • Confidence score threshold
    • Minimum variance amount
  3. Use the Search bar to find claims by patient name, claim number, or CPT code.
  4. Sort columns by clicking the column header — sort by Variance (descending) to prioritize highest-dollar underpayments.

5.5 Exporting Findings

  1. Apply any desired filters to narrow the document list.
  2. Click Export CSV (top-right of Documents page).
  3. The exported file includes: claim ID, payer, service date, CPT codes, billed amount, paid amount, expected amount, variance, confidence score, AI reasoning summary, and current appeal status.
  4. Use this export for management reporting, auditor review, or loading into your practice management system.

Section 6.0

Appeal Workflow

ServantX streamlines the appeal process from identifying an underpayment to tracking the payer's response. This section covers the full lifecycle — from reviewing a finding to entering a recovered amount in the ROI Dashboard.

6.1 The Appeal Status Ladder

Every Audit Document has an appeal status that tracks where it is in the recovery process. Move claims through this workflow as you take action.

none → identified → drafted → filed → under_review → approved / partial / denied

See Appendix B for full definitions of each status.

6.2 Step-by-Step Appeal Workflow

  1. Review the Audit Document
    Open the document from /dashboard/documents. Read the AI Reasoning section carefully. Confirm the variance amount and confidence score justify the effort. For findings under $25 or confidence below 40, consider deferring.
  2. Mark as Identified
    Change the appeal status to identified to flag this claim for appeal. This moves it into your active appeal pipeline.
  3. Generate the Appeal Letter
    Click Generate Appeal Letter. ServantX's AI (powered by GPT-4.1) drafts a formal appeal letter that includes:
    • Your hospital/practice name and the payer name
    • Claim identifiers, date of service, and member information
    • The specific fee schedule basis (e.g., Medicare RVU calculation, APC rate, DRG weight)
    • Regulatory citations: 42 CFR, applicable state Medicaid regulations
    • Your contract terms and applicable provisions
    • The exact underpayment calculation
    • A formal request for reconsideration and payment of the identified balance
  4. Review and Edit the Letter
    Read the generated letter carefully. Verify all amounts, dates, and claim references are accurate. Edit directly in the platform or download as .txt and paste into Microsoft Word to apply your letterhead.
  5. Submit to the Payer
    Submit the appeal through the payer's official channel. ServantX does not submit appeals electronically to payers — you must file through:
    • The payer's provider portal (Medicare: NGSconnect, Noridian, etc.)
    • Fax (if required by the payer)
    • Certified mail for written appeals
  6. Update Status to Filed
    After submitting, update the status to filed in ServantX. Record the submission date and reference number in the notes field.
  7. Track Payer Response
    When the payer acknowledges receipt and begins review, update to under_review. Check your clearinghouse or payer portal for responses.
  8. Record the Outcome
    When the payer responds:
    • approved — enter the full recovered amount in the Recovered Amount field
    • partial — enter the actual amount received (less than the variance)
    • denied — note the denial reason; consider escalation or second-level appeal
    The recovered amount automatically rolls into the ROI Dashboard.

Section 8.0

Projects & Audit Workspace

Projects organize formal audit runs, supporting evidence, and verification records for your organization. Every hospital account includes a default "Primary Audit Workspace" out of the box.

Navigate to: /dashboard (Projects section)

8.1 Understanding Projects

A Project is a container for an audit effort — typically tied to a specific payer, time period, or compliance initiative. Using separate projects allows your team to organize work without commingling findings from different payers or quarters.

  • The default Primary Audit Workspace is created automatically when your account is provisioned.
  • Most organizations can operate entirely within the default workspace.
  • Additional projects can be created for specialized audits (e.g., "2025 Q4 Medicare Audit" or "Aetna Contract Review — Cardiology").

8.2 Audit Evidence and Verification Runs

For compliance purposes, ServantX allows you to attach formal audit evidence and generate verification run digests — cryptographic records of the audit state at a point in time.

  1. Open a project from the dashboard.
  2. To attach evidence files (e.g., payer correspondence, contract excerpts, internal review notes), click Upload Evidence. The system generates a secure pre-signed upload URL for each file.
  3. To create a formal audit snapshot, click Run Verification. This creates a tamper-evident digest of all current findings, evidence, and timestamps — suitable for compliance documentation.
  4. Download the verification report as PDF for your compliance files.
Retention Policy
Store audit records in accordance with your organization's HIPAA retention policy — a minimum of 6 years for records related to PHI. ServantX is not a long-term records management system; export and archive completed audits per your internal policy.
Section 9.0

Analysis & Pattern Reports

Pattern Reports analyze your entire audit history to surface systemic underpayment trends — going beyond individual claims to identify CPT codes, providers, and payer behavior patterns that drive the most revenue leakage.

Navigate to: /dashboard/reports

9.1 Available Metrics

Metric Description
totalClaims Total number of claims processed across all 835 uploads
totalPaid Sum of all amounts actually paid by payers
totalExpected Sum of all expected amounts per fee schedules and contracts
totalVariance totalExpected − totalPaid. Total underpayment across all audited claims.
topCpts CPT codes ranked by total variance across all claims. Identifies your highest-impact procedure codes.
topProviders Rendering providers ranked by total underpayments. May indicate systematic denial targeting of certain providers.
topPatterns Payer + CPT + modifier combinations with repeated underpayments. These are your strongest candidates for contract disputes.
coverage Rate match coverage by engine — what percentage of billed codes were matched to an authoritative rate.

9.2 Acting on Pattern Data

  • Top CPT codes with high variance: Add these to a watch list. If the same CPT is consistently underpaid by the same payer, pull all related claims and generate a batch appeal or file a group contract dispute.
  • Top providers with frequent underpayments: Check whether a specific provider NPI is being targeted. This could indicate a credentialing gap in the payer's system.
  • Payer + CPT + modifier patterns: These are the gold standard for contract renegotiation. Document the pattern, quantify the total revenue impact, and share with your contracting team before the next renewal.
  • Low engine coverage: If coverage is under 50% for the CONTRACT engine, your uploaded contract may be missing a fee schedule addendum. Re-upload an updated version.
Monthly Review Recommendation
Run the Pattern Report at the end of each month after closing out your appeal queue. Identify the top 3 CPTs and the top 2 payers driving underpayments. Brief your billing manager with the variance totals — this data is most persuasive when presented as a cumulative dollar figure rather than per-claim amounts.

Medicare Appeal Levels

Medicare has a formal multi-level appeal process for disputed claims:

Level Name Who Reviews Filing Deadline
1st Redetermination Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) 120 days from initial determination
2nd Reconsideration Qualified Independent Contractor (QIC) 180 days from MAC decision
3rd ALJ Hearing Office of Medicare Hearings and Appeals (OMHA) 60 days from QIC decision; minimum $180 in controversy
4th Medicare Appeals Council Department Appeals Board (DAB) 60 days from ALJ decision
5th Federal District Court Federal judiciary 60 days from Council decision; minimum $1,760 in controversy
Appendix C

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a 835 and a 277?

The 835 is an Electronic Remittance Advice — it tells you what was paid and why, for claims the payer has adjudicated. The 277 is a Claim Status Inquiry/Response — it tells you the current processing status of a submitted claim. ServantX uses 835 files because underpayment analysis requires knowing the final adjudicated payment. The 277 is not used for audit purposes.

My clearinghouse doesn't export .835 files — what do I do?

Most modern clearinghouses and practice management systems support 835 export. If yours uses a different format (e.g., human-readable PDF EOBs or proprietary reports), contact support at ServantX — the team may be able to assist with conversion. Some clearinghouses require you to explicitly enroll for ERA/835 delivery; check your clearinghouse enrollment settings.

The AI flagged a claim but I believe the payer paid correctly. What do I do?

Do not file an appeal. The confidence score and AI Reasoning help distinguish solid findings from uncertain ones. If the confidence score is below 60 or the AI reasoning cites a contract provision that doesn't apply to this claim type, mark the document as none (or leave it unactioned). ServantX finds potential underpayments — your billing team's professional judgment is the final filter before an appeal is filed.

Can I use ServantX for Medicaid claims outside of Texas?

At this time, ServantX has native Texas Medicaid Fee-For-Service rates built in. For other states, set the Pricing Mode to CONTRACT and upload your state Medicaid managed care contract. The AI will audit against your contract terms. Native multi-state Medicaid rate support is on the product roadmap.

How do I handle a payer that doesn't accept electronic appeal submissions?

Download the generated appeal letter (as .txt or copy to Word) and apply your organization's letterhead. Print and submit via fax or certified mail as required by the payer. Record the fax confirmation or tracking number in the notes field of the ServantX document and set the status to filed.

The same 835 file keeps getting rejected as a duplicate. How do I re-upload it?

ServantX deduplicates by the SHA-256 hash of the file content — an identical file will always be rejected. If you need to reprocess the same data (e.g., after changing your Pricing Mode), contact your administrator for options. In most cases, if the underlying 835 data needs to be re-audited, you should download a fresh copy from your clearinghouse, which may have a slightly different timestamp in the file header.

My contract was recently renegotiated. How do I update it in ServantX?

Upload the new contract PDF under the same payer name in /dashboard/contracts. The new contract replaces the previous one for all future 835 uploads. Claims already processed before the update retain their original findings — they are not retroactively re-analyzed. If you need to re-audit old claims against new rates, re-upload those 835 files (you may need to rename them to bypass deduplication).

Who should have access to ServantX at our organization?

Access should be limited to authorized billing staff and revenue cycle personnel who have completed HIPAA training and are covered under your organization's workforce policies. Under HIPAA's minimum necessary standard, only individuals who need access to PHI for their job function should have credentials. Do not share logins between staff members — each person should have an individual account.

How does ServantX handle PHI (Protected Health Information)?

ServantX operates as a HIPAA Business Associate. PHI contained in 835 files is processed in a secure, encrypted environment. All data is encrypted in transit (TLS 1.3) and at rest (AES-256). Audit logs record all access to claim-level data. A Business Associate Agreement (BAA) must be executed before production use. Contact ServantX support for your BAA.

An appeal was approved but the payment hasn't shown up in our practice management system. What should I check?

ServantX tracks appeal status and recovered amounts based on what your staff enters manually — it does not automatically reconcile with your PM system. When you receive an approved additional payment, you must (1) enter the recovered_amount in the ServantX document, and (2) post the payment in your PM system separately. The recovered_amount in ServantX is for ROI reporting only; it does not create a transaction in your billing workflow.

Appendix D

Glossary

835
The HIPAA X12 EDI transaction set for Health Care Claim Payment/Advice. The standard electronic file format that payers use to communicate payment decisions, adjustment codes, and denial reasons to providers. Also called an ERA (Electronic Remittance Advice).
ERA
Electronic Remittance Advice. The human-friendly name for the 835 transaction. An ERA details what was paid, what was adjusted, and the reason codes for any reductions, for one or more claims.
MPFS
Medicare Physician Fee Schedule. The CMS schedule that defines reimbursement for physician and professional services billed to Medicare. Payment is calculated based on Relative Value Units (RVUs) multiplied by the Conversion Factor and adjusted for geography using the Geographic Practice Cost Index (GPCI).
IPPS
Inpatient Prospective Payment System. The Medicare payment methodology for hospital inpatient acute care stays. Payment is based on the patient's Diagnosis Related Group (DRG) weight multiplied by a hospital-specific base rate.
OPPS
Outpatient Prospective Payment System. The Medicare payment methodology for hospital outpatient services including emergency department visits, same-day surgery, observation stays, and outpatient infusion. Claims are grouped into Ambulatory Payment Classifications (APCs).
APC
Ambulatory Payment Classification. The unit of payment under OPPS. Hospital outpatient services are classified into APCs based on the procedure codes billed; each APC has a set payment rate updated annually by CMS.
DRG
Diagnosis Related Group. A classification system used by Medicare IPPS (and many commercial payers) to group inpatient hospital stays by diagnosis and procedure. Each DRG has a relative weight that determines the payment amount for that hospitalization.
RVU
Relative Value Unit. The building block of Medicare physician fee calculations. Each CPT code has three RVU components: work (physician effort), practice expense (overhead), and malpractice (liability insurance). Total RVUs are multiplied by the Conversion Factor to determine the Medicare payment amount.
GPCI
Geographic Practice Cost Index. A CMS adjustment factor applied to RVUs to account for regional variations in the cost of providing medical services. Each Medicare locality (geographic area) has its own GPCI values, which adjust the practice expense and malpractice RVU components up or down.
PHI
Protected Health Information. Under HIPAA, any individually identifiable health information — including patient names, dates of service, diagnoses, claim numbers, and any other data that could be used to identify a patient — that is transmitted or maintained by a covered entity or their business associates.
BAA
Business Associate Agreement. A HIPAA-required contract between a covered entity (your hospital or practice) and a vendor (ServantX) that handles PHI on their behalf. The BAA defines each party's responsibilities for protecting PHI. A signed BAA must be in place before using ServantX in a production environment.
CPT
Current Procedural Terminology. The standardized code set maintained by the American Medical Association (AMA) used to describe medical, surgical, and diagnostic procedures and services billed by physicians and outpatient facilities. CPT codes are 5-digit numeric codes (e.g., 99214 for an established patient office visit).
HCPCS
Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System. A code set that extends CPT codes to include supplies, equipment, and services not covered by CPT. Level I HCPCS codes are CPT codes. Level II HCPCS codes begin with a letter (A–V) and cover items such as durable medical equipment, ambulance services, and drugs.
Timely Filing
The contractual or regulatory requirement to submit a claim or appeal within a defined time window from the date of service or original remittance. Missing a timely filing deadline is an absolute bar to payment in most cases and cannot be appealed on the merits. Timely filing deadlines vary by payer and are specified in your payer contract.
Underpayment
The difference between what a payer should have paid (per the applicable fee schedule or contract) and what they actually paid. An underpayment is distinct from a denial (where no payment was made) and may occur due to incorrect rate application, missing contract provisions, payer system errors, or inappropriate downcoding.
